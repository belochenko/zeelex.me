# Problem Statement

A box of mass $M$ rests on a horizontal table. Inside the box, a body of mass $m$ is suspended from a spring of stiffness $k$. 
At what amplitude of oscillation will the box lift off the table?

# Facts
Before going into the solution, let's analyze the problem and identify the key facts from problem statement.

1. There is a box of mass $M$ resting on a horizontal table (or any horizontal surface).
2. A spring is attached to the ceiling of the box, with a body of mass m suspended from its lower end.
3. A body of mass $m$ is suspended from a spring of stiffness $k$.

Overall there are 3 bodies in the system: box, spring and mass $m$. In this problem we assume that the box is rigid and the spring is ideal. 
Also we assume that the table is rigid and system is in vacuum (no air resistance).

Initially, the system is in static equilibrium: the gravitational force is balanced by the normal reaction of the table.

# Reasoning
For the box to lift off the table, it is necessary to reduce $N$ to $0$. It means that the box cannot touch the table surface.
Therefore, the spring with mass on it must be pulled down and realeased. At that very moment, the spring exerts a compressive force on the ceiling of the box which we denote $T$,
since the spring strives to return to its equilibrium position. 

# Mathematical Solution
Equilibrium:
$$ (M + m) \cdot g = N $$
$$ \vec{F}_{box} + \vec{F}_{spring} + \vec{N} = 0 $$

Next, works with the mass on the spring. When the mass suspended from the spring is pulled down by $\Delta x_0$.

According to Hooke's law, the force exerted by the spring on the mass is:
$$ F_{spring} = k \Delta x \implies k \cdot \Delta x_0 = m \cdot g $$ 
$$ \implies \Delta x_0 = \frac{m \cdot g}{k} $$

Here $\Delta x_0$ is the **static deflection** — the equilibrium stretch of the spring 
due to the weight of the suspended mass.

# Equation of Motion

Let $x$ denote the displacement of the mass from its equilibrium position, 
positive downward. The equation of motion is:

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx $$

This is simple harmonic motion with angular frequency:

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} $$

and general solution:

$$ x(t) = A \sin(\omega t) $$

where $A$ is the oscillation amplitude.

# Lift-off Condition

At the top of the oscillation ($x = -A$), the spring is compressed 
by $(\Delta x_0 - A)$.

The forces acting on the box in the vertical direction are:

$$ N + k(A - \Delta x_0) - Mg = 0 $$

The box lifts off when $N = 0$:

$$ k(A - \Delta x_0) = Mg $$

Substituting $\Delta x_0 = \frac{mg}{k}$:

$$ kA - mg = Mg $$

$$ kA = (M + m)g $$

$$\boxed{ A = \frac{(M + m)g}{k} }$$

# Interpretation

The result has a clear physical meaning: the required amplitude is proportional 
to the total weight of the system $(M + m)g$ and inversely proportional to 
the stiffness $k$. A stiffer spring requires a larger displacement to generate 
the same force — so a softer spring lifts the box at a smaller amplitude.

**Limiting cases:**
- If $M \to 0$: $A \to \frac{mg}{k} = \Delta x_0$ — the box lifts off as soon 
  as the spring returns to its natural length.
- If $k \to \infty$: $A \to 0$ — an infinitely stiff spring transmits force 
  instantly; any oscillation lifts the box.

# Applications

The result $A = \frac{(M+m)g}{k}$ is not merely academic — it describes a 
lift-off threshold that appears across many engineering domains.

# Packaging and Transport

A fragile instrument inside a shipping box is precisely this system. 
During transport, road vibrations excite the internal mass. If the oscillation 
amplitude exceeds the threshold, the box lifts off the surface — or equivalently, 
the instrument becomes momentarily weightless inside the packaging. 

This is why sensitive equipment (medical devices, optical instruments, 
military hardware) is shipped with high-stiffness $k$ foam inserts: 
a larger $k$ raises the lift-off threshold.

# Vibration Isolation

The same physics appears in reverse: engineers designing vibration isolators 
for industrial machinery (compressors, pumps, CNC machines) must ensure 
that $N > 0$ at all times. If the machine's internal oscillating components 
reach the critical amplitude, the machine begins to "walk" or jump — 
a failure mode known as **dynamic uplift**.

# Seismic Engineering

A building on a foundation is structurally analogous. During an earthquake, 
internal floors and mechanical systems oscillate. The lift-off condition 
determines whether the structure separates from its foundation — 
a catastrophic failure mode in seismic design.

# Railway Dynamics

A freight wagon on suspension springs carries an oscillating load. 
The condition $N = 0$ corresponds to **wheel lift-off** — the wheel 
loses contact with the rail. This is a critical safety threshold 
in railway engineering, directly governed by the same inequality:

$$ A < \frac{(M+m)g}{k} $$

# Jumping Robots

Some biomimetic robots exploit this effect deliberately. An internal 
mass-spring system is driven to the critical amplitude, causing the 
robot body to lift off without any external leg mechanism. 
The lift-off amplitude formula directly determines the required 
spring stiffness and stroke for a given robot mass.
